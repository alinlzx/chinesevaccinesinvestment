Chinese Vaccines and Investment

Advait Arun & Alex Lin

11/16/2021

Research Question

Do Chinese vaccine distribution patterns track recent Chinese investment patterns?

The Chinese government is sending Chinese vaccines, made primarily by the companies Sinovac and Sinopharm, around the world. The news portrays most recipient countries as developing nations, many of which arent able to pay for Western-made Pfizer and Moderna vaccines on their own. The United States government sometimes portrays Chinese vaccine donations as self-serving vaccine diplomacy that will end up boosting China. If this is true, China would be sending vaccines to its economic and political allies to bolster goodwill and to reinforce their dependence on China.

Hypothesis: We expect Chinese vaccine disbursement to be higher in countries with higher amounts of Chinese investment.

For this purpose, weve used Chinese investment over the past five years (since 2016).

Data

For Chinese investment, we got our data from the American Enterprise Institute. It goes all the way back to 2000 and includes lots of industry classifications. It also labels whether or not investment recipient countries are part of Chinas Belt and Road Initiative.

For Chinese vaccines, we got our data from Bridge Consultants, a Beijing-based economic development consultant group. They maintain a Chinese vaccine tracker, and we owe them a debt of gratidute for allowing us to use their data. All vaccine data is as of October 26th.

Later, we look at US Investment Data since 2016. Our data, also segregated by industry, comes from the Bureau of Economic Analysis.

Our FullData spreadsheet combines Chinese investment data, Chinese vaccine disbursement data, and US investment data. We use bi_class to create bivariate class mappings for each pair of the three variables in order to create bivariate chloropleths. We also have each variable in per-million-people format: Chinese vaccines per million people in recipient country, Chinese investment per million people, and US investment per million people. Finally, we join that spreadsheet with the world map.

Theres one issue with our bivariate classing. Normally, the bi_class function breaks up observations into quantiles. But because our data has so many zeros, we had to break up our observations differently. We set the style argument of bi_class to jenks: the jenks method of breaking up data finds natural breaks in the numbers where drawing a line seems best. Alex and I are trusting the computer here we couldnt figure out a way to make quantiles work no matter how we tried. We believe its because we just have too many zeros in the data.
